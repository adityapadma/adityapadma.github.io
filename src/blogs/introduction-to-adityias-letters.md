> All right, people, let's do this one last time. My name is Aditya Padmagirwar. I was not bitten by a radioactive spider, and for the last 4 years, I thought I was the one and only person who would start writing my Substack and then just not post[^1].

Mostly because I felt I was cringe or just my imposter syndrome, thinking my essays on the things I observe and see in the world would never really be interesting to anyone. But after 4 years of procrastinating, I am going to try to stick to a bi-weekly posting schedule.

## How I Got Here

I have been an avid reader of Newsletters ever since I got unsupervised access to the internet. I would read people's digital garden blogs and watch video essays on YouTube. This medium of using short, long-form content to process information or research work has always made me want to do something similar. I would write something, some idea for a video essay or a blog that I can develop, but never really come around to completing it.

I started reading Austin Kleon's Substack[^2] when Substack was just beginning to take off (back when Substack didn't have any gimmicks, just a plain old newsletter service with only writing tools). Afterwards, I saw a senior at my college with his Substack *Audience of One*[^3], where he would just post his self-improvement journey in the form of journal blogs. That's when I decided to do something like a blog where I write in this autoethnographic way to capture the world we are living in right now. So I started this Substack and called it Aditya's Letters back in 2023; I would start writing and never come around to completing them.

## What the f*** is Autoethnography? (Source: Trust me bro)

I have hated Social Media since the start. *Hate. Hate. Hate* small form of content (even though I love consuming it. My 4 hours everyday average on Instagram is evidence for that). But lately, I have been capturing more, taking more notes, taking pictures of the life around me and capturing the nuances of the world we live in. So maybe this practice of writing and capturing could help me find my research topics and get some feedback on my writing.

Right Now, as my College Life comes to an end, over the last few months, I have started writing again. God, I am writing again. It is an amazing feeling when you bring your thoughts to paper or screen and see your idea get some shape or form.

## Aditya's Letters

So yes, I have started to write again and want to create a proper outlet for it. And this Substack *Aditya's Letters* will be the outlet for it. Named it Aditya's Letter because in high school, I used to have this habit of writing a massive wall of text in the form of letters on people's birthdays or when I used to have random ideas about wanting to create something, say a science project or a game, I would write it in the form of Essay talking to somebody, like a Letter addressed to no one. That is what I think Aditya's letters are; they are just letters addressed to no one in particular but are just a way to document the world.

Now, over the summer, you should expect a weekly letter in which I will talk about:

- **my current research**, some interesting papers or studies I read
- **my everchanging obsessions with all forms of Media** — maybe movies, maybe science fiction or just me ranting about how good the Delhi metro is and how cities all over India, if they just invest in good public transport, will become far more liveable than it is now
- **my current playlist that I am listening to**
- and more things to come

> **PS.** All the media I refer to in the body will have a link for them in the footnotes. Also, If you read till here, reply below 'General Kenobi' + your favourite emoji this week.

## May 2025 Letter

This whole month was the month of Goodbyes and Farewells. My work took a backseat, and I couldn't read a lot (or after the farewell party last week, my brain has reset, and I don't remember studying for the Finals at all.) But I did come across this really intresting video essay by Marcus Verner on How the show *The Expanse*[^4] gives us a glimpse into the post-work post-everything-automated world that we are running after. This ties in perfectly with 2 courses I took this semester: Philosophy of Technology[^5] and Ethics in AI[^6].

The Expanse is one of my favourite science fiction shows about the near future of Human Civilization, where we are interplanetary species and how capitalism manifests itself on a cosmic level. Really interesting to watch if you have enough time to binge through it, but the book is far better at world-building. But this video essay by Marcus goes deep into what happens to society when every work we do as humans can be automated? How do regular humans sustain themselves and find meaning when there is no work to be done?
