KaavadBits: Reimagining a 400-Year-Old Storytelling Tradition

An exploration in Tangible Interactive Storytelling, presented at ACM TEI '24. Bridging the gap between ancient Rajasthani oral heritage and modern multi-sensory interfaces.

0. Kaavad Baachana

“Story is perhaps the oldest form of communication known to humankind. It has a way of mesmerising the listeners into silence and the tellers into expressing the deepest desires and anxieties of their society, directly or through subversive means." - Nina Sabnani in ‘The Kaavad storytelling tradition of Rajasthan’

Kaavad is a portable wooden storytelling shrine from Rajasthan, historically carried by a traveling narrator known as the kaavadiya. Its hinged doors, called kivads, open layer by layer to reveal scenes, characters, and episodes as the performance unfolds.

Kaavad baachana (also referred to as kaavad baanchana) is the oral act of reciting these stories to patrons. The tradition is participatory and performative: voice, gesture, object handling, and audience engagement are all integral to the storytelling method.

Why This Matters

KaavadBits is designed as a contemporary continuation of this tradition: it retains the narrator-patron relationship and ritual structure, while translating it into a tangible, interactive format for present-day audiences.

Citations

  1. [25] Nina Sabnani. 2009. The Kaavad storytelling tradition of Rajasthan. (2009).

Design Process

We adopted a Research through Design (RtD)[1] methodology to conceptualise KaavadBits, evaluating ideas through numerous hand-sketches and physical cardboard prototypes.

Early iterations experimented with highly complex mechanical interactions. For instance, we devised a hand-cranked system modeled after a bioscope, which was intended to let users physically "wind time backward" to return to divergent points and remake their narrative choices.

However, rapid pilot testing with these cardboard models quickly revealed that managing a bidirectional flow of time was cognitively overwhelming for users. Furthermore, reversing timelines felt conceptually alien to traditional linear Indian storytelling structures. This crucial insight allowed us to pivot and simplify the interaction model for the final build, ensuring the experience felt intuitive and culturally faithful.

Citations

  1. [1] Zimmerman, J. et al. (2007). Research through design as a method for interaction design research in HCI.
    Note: Research through Design (RtD) is an approach where the act of designing—prototyping, testing, and iterating—is itself the primary method of inquiry used to generate new academic knowledge.

1. Installation Overview

KaavadBits is a tabletop storytelling installation that embodies the narrator-patron perspective of kaavad baanchana, a Rajasthani storytelling tradition dating back roughly 400 years.[1] This explainer-led walkthrough introduces the installation's components and demonstrates how users participate in the narrative flow.

The project reframes the traditional kaavad performance as an interactive stage where narration, tangible objects, and responsive motion work together. Rather than only observing a story, users progressively unlock, activate, and shape key narrative moments through physical interaction.[1]

Narrative Intent

The installation preserves the performative spirit of oral storytelling while giving contemporary audiences agency: users do not just listen, they co-author the unfolding experience through touch, selection, and choice.

2. Installation Components

The kaavadiya (narrator) is portrayed as a tree so that the narrator is integrated into the story world, since most Panchatantra tales are set in a forest.[1] A servo-motor-enabled canopy oscillates during narration to direct audience attention, while an LED matrix running through the branches lights up the currently chosen path.

The canopy also houses special figurines and interaction tokens (apple, mango, pear, and the Bloom of Eternal Tales). A pair of hanging roots allows users to trigger binary branching choices at critical narrative moments.[1]

The traditional kivads (doors) are reimagined to represent complete stories and their character sets. The artefact uses four NFC-enabled, laser-cut MDF story blocks and eight character figurines drawn from Panchatantra, with a stage backdrop for images, captions, and prompts. At the center, an oscillating platform with an NFC scanner reacts when contextually important characters are placed on it.[1]

3. Interaction Task Flow

The interaction begins when users open the kivads, explore the available stories and characters, and reveal the stage area. The narrator then greets the audience and requests a story card with its characters as patronage to begin the enactment.[1]

Narration and Activation Loop

After receiving the Bloom of Eternal Tales token, the narrator begins performing the selected story. Users continue interacting by placing and replacing character figurines on the central platform at prompted moments.

This interaction serves two purposes at once: it gives users meaningful agency and keeps audience attention synchronized with narrative emphasis, especially during character dialogue and conflict scenes.[1]

Choice-Making Moments

At diverging points, the narrator asks the patron to decide between two options. Users pull one of the hanging roots to commit to a branch:

  • Left root: choose the first narrative option.
  • Right root: choose the alternate option.

4. User Study & Future Work

We conducted a pilot study to evaluate the installation's tangible interface and its efficacy as a cultural preservation agent.

Excellent Usability

The system achieved a strong System Usability Scale (SUS) score of 78.75, proving the physical interaction model to be intuitive and engaging for first-time users.

Bridging the Cultural Gap

While nearly all users were familiar with digital interactive storytelling, 75% had no prior knowledge of the traditional Kaavad. The installation successfully sparked high interest in exploring more heritage-based art.

Pivoting to Pedagogy

Participants enjoyed the novelty but warned that long-term engagement might wane. This crucial insight shifted our focus toward adapting the system as an interactive pedagogical tool for children, who naturally benefit from folklore repetition learning.

Future Directions

  • Dynamic Narratives: Fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) to dynamically remix and output unique storylines tailored to the physical KaavadBits story tree.
  • Museum Integration: Evolving the tabletop setup into a fully ambient, inclusive museum experience with universal sound and imagery.
